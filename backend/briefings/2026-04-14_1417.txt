Stablecoins sit at the intersection of traditional money and cryptocurrency. They aim to offer the speed and flexibility of crypto without the price swings that often define it. But “stable” doesn’t mean risk-free, and understanding how these assets work is essential before putting money into them. Stablecoins are a type of cryptocurrency (or token) designed to maintain a steady value. Most are “pegged” to a traditional currency, such as the U.S. dollar, at a 1:1 ratio. That means one unit of a dollar-backed stablecoin aims to always equal $1. Unlike traditional cryptos, which can move sharply in price within hours, stablecoins aim to reduce volatility. They do this by backing each token with other assets or by using mechanisms that control supply and demand. For instance, specialized software can increase or decrease the number of tokens in circulation to help stabilize the price. Read more:Why you might one day use stablecoins in place of credit cards or bank accounts To understand how stablecoins work, you need to learn how they maintain their price. In the case of a stablecoin tied to the dollar, the peg to $1 is maintained through a mix of reserves and market incentives. Key terms to help you understand how stablecoins work: Issuers: Companies that create stablecoins and manage the reserves. Reserves: Assets held by the issuer, such as cash or short-term government bonds, that support the value of the tokens. Users: Individuals or institutions that buy, hold, send, or receive stablecoins. Exchanges: Online platforms where people trade cryptocurrencies. Blockchain networks: The systems that record and verify crypto transactions. There are four main stablecoin models: Fiat-backed, crypto-backed, commodity-backed, and algorithmic. Here’s how the process typically works for fiat-backed stablecoins: 1. Issuance A user sends traditional currency, such as U.S. dollars, to the stablecoin issuer. This usually happens through a bank transfer or a crypto exchange. In return, the issuer creates (a.k.a. “mints”) an equivalent amount of stablecoins and sends them to the user to store in their crypto wallet. 2. Circulation Once issued, stablecoins can be used like any other cryptocurrency. Users can transfer them to other people, trade them on exchanges, or hold them as a store of value. All transactions will be recorded on the blockchain. 3. Redemption If a user wants to convert stablecoins back into cash, they’ll send the tokens back to the issuer. The issuer then “burns” the tokens, which means they’re removed from circulation, and returns the equivalent amount of traditional currency. Not all stablecoins follow this exact model. Crypto-backed stablecoins use other cryptocurrencies as collateral; commodity-backed tokens are linked to physical assets like gold; and algorithmic stablecoins use software to adjust supply without holding traditional reserves. These models can be more complex and may carry a higher risk, especially during periods of market stress. Moving money between exchanges Crypto prices can change quickly. If you want to switch between assets or platforms, timing matters. Stablecoins can make this easier. Instead of withdrawing money from crypto holdings to a bank account, which can take days, you can convert it into a stablecoin and move it between exchanges within minutes. This avoids the need to convert crypto to a fiat currency and other potential bank-related delays. 2. Holding value without holding cash In traditional finance, you might sell an investment and hold cash in a bank account. In crypto, stablecoins serve a similar role. They let you step out of more volatile assets without leaving the crypto market entirely. This can be useful during periods of sharp price swings. An investor might sell a volatile asset, move into stablecoins, and wait for clearer conditions before re-entering the market. Again, this reduces the need to move money back and forth between a bank and a crypto platform, which can involve fees and delays. 3.Sending cross-border payments Sending money across national borders through traditional banking systems can be slow and expensive. Transfers may pass through multiple intermediaries, each adding fees and processing times. Stablecoins offer an alternative.
