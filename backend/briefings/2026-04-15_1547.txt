There’s no growth for RIAs without talent, but it takes a concerted effort to lure and retain top advisors and operators, according to panelists speaking at RIA Edge Nashville on Tuesday. Mike LaMena, CEO of $580 billion New York-based RIA Wealthspire, said the top driver of growth for his acquisitive firm is good advisors and, increasingly, leaders across other functions of themulti-service organization. “We are in a talent-based industry, so if you want to grow and you’re not going to start with your strategy relative to people, you’re going to be failing,” LaMena said. “The talent we have goes up and down the elevators, in and out the doors every day.” Steward Partners CEO Jim Gold, a firm withmore than $50 billion in client assets, agreed, noting that his Stamford, Conn.-based firm was built from the early days of cold-calling clients to a model of bringing on top breakaway advisors who were passionate and dedicated to financial advice. Today, however, Gold said the focus of employees and firms has shifted, and RIAs need to adjust. “When I started in 1995, there was a singular skill you needed—you had to sell. You were a hunter,” Gold said. “This generation today, they’re not hunters, they’re farmers. Someone else built this huge farm, and they need somebody to be caretakers now.” In a separate panel, Dennis Morton, the founder and senior wealth manager of his own firm, Morton Brown Family Wealth, served as an example of what happens when RIAs don’t pay attention to talent. “We were part of a failed succession plan,” Morton said.&nbsp; Morton said he and his now-partner in the firm were approaching their 40s, but didn’t see a culture of growth or drive from the firm’s founder, so they decided to set out on their own. “We were looking at the next 10 years of our careers and saying, ‘Do we really want to sit back and not do the things that need to be done to make this a sustainable, growing business that we are going to take over and wait for this person to retire and then jump through flaming hoops in our 50s to make it happen?’” he said. Instead, the duo set off on their own and created a firm with a strategic plan that has grown them to about 300 households and $520 million in assets under management. Henry Hagenbach, senior managing director, mergers and acquisitions, for $42 billion RIA Lido Advisors, noted the “endemic issue” in the industry of finding and retaining top talent. At Los Angeles-based Lido, he said the firm has found success by beingselective in its M&A decisions&nbsp;and by giving employees a stake in the firm’s growth. “The way we’ve tried to solve for [talent] is to equitize early and often,” Hagenbach said. “At our last company meeting, we were about 450 employees, and 74% of them are shareholders.” The results have helped the firm achieve rapid growth. Hagenbach noted that, when he joined the firm about two years ago, he was employee number 200. Now there are more than 450 people. “It’s all about finding the right talent and then betting on them,” he said. “Team cohesion is also paramount to all of this, so a rising tide can lift all ships.” LaMena of Wealthspire said there’s a persistent challenge for the wealth management industry in attracting young people, partly because they may not see a long-term career path. “I think if we want to be successful in either of those places, we have a responsibility to ultimately lay out well-defined career paths to give people visibility into how I actually enter the industry, and how I can progress in the industry,” he said.&nbsp; Steward CEO Gold said he sees a misperception problem for the industry that might turn people away. “The problem of our industry is society sees Wall Street—Boiler Room, The Wolf of Wall Street,” he said. “They don’t make movies about people in this room who go to work every day, who work hard and care about their clients. So I think we need more role modeling.” Gold and LaMena spoke to their firm’s differing models for growing large RIAs.&nbsp; Gold characterized Steward as a “sell and stay” model, in which sellers to the firm maintain control, branding and client relationship management while leveraging the scale of the larger firm. Wealthspire, on the other hand, is a full-integration model with back-office centralization to scale and achieve consistency in work across advisors and clients. Both CEOs agreed, however, that as their firms have grown, so have the opportunities for people beyond just advisors to have lifelong careers. “I think [RIA growth] is actually a huge benefit, because it’s not just the advisor pathway,” he said. “There are people that build amazing careers in marketing, finance, supporting the industry and supporting the businesses that we’re involved in. … We just have to tell the story in ways that get people excited about joining it.” Weʼre unable to load stories right now.
